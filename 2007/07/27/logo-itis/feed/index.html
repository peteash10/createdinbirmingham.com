Firstly, I agree with your comments about the design of the banner. As a funder we do request that our support is publicly acknowledged but we don’t really specify that it has to be on everything. I have an auditor to keep happy once a year and they want to see what our money has been spent on. My mistake is not to make this clear. I should really give better advice to the organisers of events we support. I think it would go something like this:
“Digital Central (and its funder, Advantage West Midlands) have a requirement to see our support acknowledged. You can indicate our support through displaying our logo on your website or on printed materials. You do not need to display it on all items related to the event. In particular, if you feel that the inclusion of our logo will unnecessarily interfere with the design of your publicity material then let us know and we can agree an alternative form of acknowledgement.”

I think I’ll add that text to the ‘Logos and Branding’ guidelines on our website (just have in fact!). As long as I can keep the auditor happy then I’m happy basically.

One final point though. There may be a political reason to show this many logos. It may influence those wavering about funding this event in future years to be convinced that it is something that lots of key people are buying into so they should to.

]]> By: Stef Lewandowski http://www.createdinbirmingham.com/2007/07/27/logo-itis/#comment-587 Fri, 27 Jul 2007 11:06:43 +0000 http://www.createdinbirmingham.com/?p=481#comment-587 Hi Pete,

I wrote about this last year in an article for Ten4 magazine.

http://alt.3form.net/?p=75

I always push for not including logos in excessive numbers in print work. On the web it’s easy to have a “Sponsors” page, or to be creative and have each sponsor taking ownership of a particular section of a site, with associated _content_.

In print it usually comes down to one sponsor (or usually funding provider) saying “it’s a requirement of our funding that our logo is included in x,y and z at such-and-such a size.”

When you get to the level of an international event like Rhubarb you attract more and more sponsors, collaborators, funders and supporters. When one of the funding providers gets to have their logo on something, then the argument is obvious. “If X gets their logo included in x,y and z then we have to include everyone”.

The result is logo-itis.

Possible solutions:

1. Say no to excessive logo inclusion.
2. Use the Web for sponsorship information.
3. Find more interesting (and embedded) ways for brands to gain exposure.
4. Don’t promise “your logo included in all of our print” as part of the sponsorship deal.
5. Attach sponsorship to specific elements – eg. The opening party sponsored by X, the champagne, sponsored by Y, the keynote address sponsored by Z, etc.

This isn’t an isolated case, but in the process of organising a major event like Rhubarb I don’t think that this would have been a major consideration! I’m sure they have a load of other things to be dealing with…
